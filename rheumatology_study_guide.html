1. Rheumatoid Arthritis Common

Epidemiology & Pathophysiology

  • Prevalence: 0.5-1% of population, F:M = 3:1
  • Peak onset: 30-50 years (but can occur at any age)
  • Pathophysiology: Autoimmune synovitis ‚Üí pannus formation ‚Üí cartilage/bone destruction
  • Genetic association: HLA-DR4 (shared epitope), PTPN22
  • Environmental triggers: Smoking (strongest modifiable risk factor), periodontal disease, silica exposure

Clinical Presentation

MCP/PIP/Wrist ‚Äî RA loves the small joints of hands (spares DIPs!)

Joint Involvement

  • Classic pattern: Symmetric polyarthritis of small joints
  • Hands: MCP, PIP (spares DIP)
  • Wrists: Very common, early involvement
  • Feet: MTP joints
  • Other: Knees, ankles, elbows, shoulders
  • Cervical spine: C1-C2 subluxation (check before intubation!)
  • Morning stiffness: >1 hour (inflammatory pattern)

Extra-Articular Manifestations

Felty syndrome (RA + splenomegaly + neutropenia) increases infection risk. These patients need aggressive RA treatment.

Imaging

  • X-ray (early): Soft tissue swelling, periarticular osteopenia, joint space narrowing
  • X-ray (late): Erosions (marginal), subluxations, deformities
  • Ultrasound: Synovitis, erosions (more sensitive than X-ray early)
  • MRI: Bone marrow edema, early erosions, synovitis

Treatment

Treatment Goals

  • Treat-to-target: Aim for remission or low disease activity
  • Window of opportunity: Early aggressive treatment prevents joint damage
  • Monitoring: DAS28, CDAI, or SDAI scores every 3-6 months

First-Line: csDMARDs

Methotrexate (MTX) ‚Äî Anchor drug for RA
  • Dose: Start 7.5-15mg weekly, titrate to 20-25mg weekly
  • Folate: Folic acid 1mg daily (reduces side effects)
  • Monitoring: CBC, CMP, LFTs at baseline, then every 2-4 weeks initially, then every 8-12 weeks
  • Contraindications: Pregnancy (teratogenic), liver disease, heavy alcohol use, severe renal impairment
  • Side effects: GI upset, stomatitis, hepatotoxicity, cytopenias, pneumonitis

Prognosis & Monitoring

  • Poor prognostic factors: High-titer RF, anti-CCP positive, early erosions, extra-articular disease, high disease activity
  • CV risk: RA increases CV mortality 1.5-2x; treat traditional risk factors aggressively
  • Remission rates: 20-40% achieve sustained remission with modern therapy

  • Sun protection: Sunscreen, protective clothing
  • Cardiovascular risk reduction: Aggressive management
  • Vaccinations: Annual flu, pneumococcal (no live vaccines if immunosuppressed)

3. Gout & Pseudogout Common

Gout (Monosodium Urate Crystal Disease)

Risk Factors

  • Hyperuricemia causes: Underexcretion (90%) vs overproduction (10%)
  • Diet: Red meat, organ meats, shellfish, alcohol (especially beer), fructose
  • Medications: Thiazides, loop diuretics, low-dose aspirin, cyclosporine
  • Comorbidities: CKD, metabolic syndrome, obesity, HTN

Clinical Stages

  1. Asymptomatic hyperuricemia: No treatment indicated (most never develop gout)
  2. Acute gouty arthritis: Sudden, severe monoarticular inflammation
  3. Intercritical gout: Asymptomatic periods between attacks
  4. Chronic tophaceous gout: Tophi, chronic arthritis, joint destruction

Acute Gout Presentation

  • Classic: Podagra (1st MTP) ‚Äî 50% of first attacks
  • Character: Rapid onset (peaks 12-24h), exquisitely tender, erythema, warmth
  • Other sites: Ankle, knee, wrist, elbow
  • Triggers: Dietary indiscretion, alcohol, dehydration, surgery, illness, medication changes
Serum uric acid may be NORMAL during acute attack (consumed into crystals). Don't use acute level to rule out gout!

Diagnosis

Gold Standard: Arthrocentesis showing negatively birefringent, needle-shaped MSU crystals under polarized light microscopy

Pseudogout (CPPD Crystal Arthritis)

  • Crystals: Calcium pyrophosphate dihydrate
  • Classic joints: Knee (most common), wrist, shoulder
  • Imaging: Chondrocalcinosis (cartilage calcification) on X-ray
  • Associations: Hemochromatosis, hyperparathyroidism, hypothyroidism, hypomagnesemia

Treatment: Same as gout (NSAIDs, colchicine, steroids). No urate-lowering equivalent exists for CPPD.

4. Polymyalgia Rheumatica & Giant Cell Arteritis Time-Sensitive

Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)

Key Features

  • Age: Almost always >50 years (peak 70-80)
  • Symptoms: Bilateral shoulder and hip girdle pain/stiffness
  • Morning stiffness: >45 minutes
  • Onset: Often sudden (patients can identify exact day)
  • Constitutional: Fatigue, malaise, low-grade fever, weight loss
  • NO true weakness: Pain limits function, but strength is normal
PMR causes pain, not weakness. If true proximal muscle weakness exists, think inflammatory myopathy and check CK.

Diagnosis

  • ESR: Usually >40 mm/h (often >100)
  • CRP: Elevated
  • CK: Normal (distinguishes from myositis)
  • RF, Anti-CCP: Negative
  • Ultrasound: Bilateral subacromial/subdeltoid bursitis, biceps tenosynovitis, hip synovitis
Dramatic steroid response: Improvement within 24-72 hours of starting prednisone is virtually diagnostic. If no response, reconsider diagnosis.

Treatment

  • Prednisone: 12.5-25 mg daily (NOT high-dose like GCA)
  • Taper: Slowly over 1-2 years
    • Reduce by 2.5mg every 2-4 weeks until 10mg
    • Below 10mg: reduce by 1mg every 4-8 weeks
  • Relapse: Common (30-50%), especially below 10mg
  • Steroid-sparing: Methotrexate, tocilizumab if relapsing

Giant Cell Arteritis (Temporal Arteritis)

GCA is an OPHTHALMOLOGIC EMERGENCY. Untreated, can cause permanent blindness within hours to days. Start steroids IMMEDIATELY if suspected ‚Äî do not wait for biopsy.

Clinical Features

  • Age: >50 years (same as PMR)
  • Headache: New-onset, often temporal
  • Scalp tenderness: Pain when combing hair
  • Jaw claudication: Pain with chewing (highly specific)
  • Visual symptoms: Amaurosis fugax, diplopia, vision loss
  • Temporal artery: Tender, thickened, nodular, decreased pulse
  • PMR symptoms: Present in 40-60% of GCA patients
GCA Red Flags ‚Äî JAW claudication, Amaurosis/visual changes, Headache (new)

Diagnosis

  • ESR: Usually very high (>50, often >100 mm/h)
  • CRP: Elevated
  • Temporal artery biopsy: Gold standard ‚Äî granulomatous inflammation with giant cells. Get 2-3cm segment; consider bilateral. Remains positive for 2-6 weeks after starting steroids
  • Temporal artery ultrasound: "Halo sign" (hypoechoic ring) is suggestive

Treatment

ScenarioTreatment
GCA without visual symptomsPrednisone 40-60 mg daily (1 mg/kg)
GCA with visual symptomsIV methylprednisolone 1g daily √ó 3 days, then high-dose oral
MaintenanceSlow taper over 1-2 years; add aspirin 81mg daily
Relapsing/steroid-sparingTocilizumab (GiACTA trial), methotrexate
10-30% of PMR patients have GCA. Always ask about headache, jaw claudication, and visual symptoms at every PMR visit!

Large Vessel GCA

  • Can involve aorta and major branches
  • Presents with arm claudication, subclavian/axillary bruits, BP discrepancy between arms
  • Risk of aortic aneurysm/dissection ‚Äî screen with CTA or PET-CT
  • May present without cranial symptoms (biopsy-negative)

5. Spondyloarthropathies Boards Favorite

Overview

Group of inflammatory arthritides sharing clinical features:

  • Axial involvement: Sacroiliitis, spondylitis
  • Peripheral arthritis: Asymmetric, oligoarticular
  • Enthesitis: Inflammation at tendon/ligament insertions
  • Dactylitis: "Sausage digits"
  • Extra-articular: Uveitis, psoriasis, inflammatory bowel disease
  • RF negative (seronegative arthropathies)
  • HLA-B27 association

Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS)

Clinical Features

  • Demographics: Young men (20-40), M:F = 3:1
  • Cardinal symptom: Inflammatory back pain
    • Onset <40 years
    • Insidious onset
    • Morning stiffness >30 minutes
    • Improves with exercise, worsens with rest
    • Nocturnal pain (wakes from sleep)
  • Sacroiliitis: Alternating buttock pain
  • Decreased spinal mobility: Positive Schober test (normal ‚â•5 cm)
  • Chest expansion: Reduced (<5 cm at nipple level)

Extra-Articular Manifestations

SystemManifestationFrequency
EyeAcute anterior uveitis (AAU)25-40%
CardiacAortic regurgitation, conduction defects2-10%
PulmonaryApical fibrosis (late)Rare
GIIBD (microscopic or overt)5-10%
RenalIgA nephropathy, AA amyloidosisRare
Acute anterior uveitis (AAU) is the most common extra-articular manifestation of AS. Presents with acute, unilateral red eye with pain and photophobia. HLA-B27 positive in >50% of AAU.

Imaging

  • X-ray sacroiliac joints: Erosions ‚Üí sclerosis ‚Üí fusion (bilateral symmetric)
  • X-ray spine: Squaring of vertebrae, syndesmophytes, "bamboo spine" (late)
  • MRI: Bone marrow edema at sacroiliac joints (early disease before X-ray changes)

Treatment

  • Exercise: Essential ‚Äî maintain spinal mobility, swimming excellent
  • NSAIDs: First-line, continuous use may slow radiographic progression
  • TNF inhibitors: Highly effective for refractory disease
  • IL-17 inhibitors: Secukinumab, ixekizumab
  • JAK inhibitors: Tofacitinib, upadacitinib (newer option)
  • csDMARDs (MTX, SSZ): NOT effective for axial disease; may help peripheral arthritis

Psoriatic Arthritis (PsA)

Clinical Patterns

  1. Asymmetric oligoarthritis (most common, 30-50%)
  2. Symmetric polyarthritis (RA-like, 15-25%)
  3. DIP arthritis (5-10%) ‚Äî associated with nail changes
  4. Spondylitis/sacroiliitis (5-20%)
  5. Arthritis mutilans (<5%) ‚Äî destructive, "pencil-in-cup"

Key Features

  • Dactylitis: "Sausage digit" ‚Äî entire finger/toe swollen
  • Enthesitis: Achilles tendinitis, plantar fasciitis
  • Nail changes: Pitting, onycholysis, oil spots ‚Äî correlate with DIP involvement
  • Skin: Psoriasis (skin changes may precede, follow, or be absent)
15-20% of PsA patients have no visible psoriasis. Check scalp, umbilicus, gluteal cleft, and nails carefully!

Treatment

DomainTreatment Options
Peripheral arthritisMTX, leflunomide, SSZ, apremilast, TNFi, IL-17i, IL-23i, JAKi
Axial diseaseNSAIDs, TNFi, IL-17i, JAKi (NOT MTX)
Enthesitis/dactylitisNSAIDs, TNFi, IL-17i, IL-23i
SkinIL-17i, IL-23i often preferred

Reactive Arthritis (Formerly Reiter's Syndrome)

  • Trigger: Post-infectious (GI: Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, Yersinia; GU: Chlamydia)
  • Timing: 1-4 weeks after infection
  • Classic triad: Arthritis + urethritis + conjunctivitis ("can't see, can't pee, can't climb a tree")
  • Other: Keratoderma blennorrhagicum, circinate balanitis, oral ulcers
  • Treatment: NSAIDs, treat underlying infection if persistent; DMARDs for chronic

Enteropathic Arthritis

  • Associated with: Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis
  • Types:
    • Peripheral (parallels bowel activity) ‚Äî treat the gut
    • Axial (independent of bowel activity) ‚Äî treat like AS
  • Caution: NSAIDs may flare IBD; TNFi (infliximab, adalimumab) preferred

Clinical Manifestations

Skin

  • Raynaud's phenomenon: Almost universal (>95%), often first symptom
  • Skin thickening: Starts in fingers, may progress proximally
  • Digital ulcers: Painful, can become infected
  • Sclerodactyly: Tight, shiny skin on fingers
  • Calcinosis: Subcutaneous calcium deposits
  • Telangiectasias: Face, hands

GI (>90% of patients)

  • Small bowel: Malabsorption, bacterial overgrowth, pseudo-obstruction
  • Colon: Constipation, wide-mouth diverticula

  • ILD: More common in dcSSc, anti-Scl-70 positive
  • PAH: More common in lcSSc, anti-centromere positive

Scleroderma renal crisis: Acute hypertensive emergency with AKI. Occurs in 10-15% of dcSSc, often within first 4 years. Risk factors: diffuse skin disease, anti-RNA polymerase III, corticosteroid use (>15mg/day).

Treatment: ACE inhibitors (captopril) ‚Äî even if creatinine rises initially. Continue even if dialysis needed. Do NOT use calcium channel blockers initially.

Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA, formerly Wegener's)

  • Classic triad: Upper airway + Lower airway + Kidney
  • Upper airway: Chronic sinusitis, saddle nose deformity, subglottic stenosis
  • Lungs: Nodules (can cavitate), hemorrhage, infiltrates
  • Kidney: Pauci-immune crescentic GN (RPGN)
  • Other: Scleritis, mononeuritis multiplex
  • ANCA: PR3/c-ANCA (80-90%)

Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA)

  • Similar to GPA but: No granulomas, no upper airway disease
  • Pulmonary-renal syndrome: Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage + RPGN
  • ANCA: MPO/p-ANCA (60-70%)

Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (EGPA, formerly Churg-Strauss)

  • Key feature: Asthma + eosinophilia + vasculitis
  • Phases: Allergic (asthma, rhinitis) ‚Üí Eosinophilic (infiltrates) ‚Üí Vasculitic
  • Cardiac: Eosinophilic myocarditis (leading cause of death)
  • ANCA: MPO/p-ANCA (40-60%)

Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN)

  • Medium vessel: Aneurysms at branch points, NO glomerulonephritis (spares small vessels)
  • Features: Livedo reticularis, skin nodules, mononeuritis multiplex, testicular pain, mesenteric ischemia
  • Association: Hepatitis B (treat the HBV!)
  • ANCA: Negative
  • Imaging: Mesenteric angiography shows microaneurysms

IgA Vasculitis (Henoch-Sch√∂nlein Purpura)

  • Classic: Children after URI
  • Tetrad: Palpable purpura (buttocks, legs) + arthritis + abdominal pain + nephritis
  • Usually self-limited: Supportive care, steroids for severe GI/renal

8. Inflammatory Myopathies

Classification

  • Dermatomyositis (DM)
  • Polymyositis (PM)
  • Immune-mediated necrotizing myopathy (IMNM)
  • Inclusion body myositis (IBM)
  • Antisynthetase syndrome

Dermatomyositis

Skin Findings

  • Heliotrope rash: Violaceous discoloration of eyelids
  • Gottron's papules: Erythematous papules over MCP/PIP/DIP (pathognomonic)
  • Gottron's sign: Same distribution but macular
  • Shawl sign: V-neck and upper back erythema
  • Mechanic's hands: Cracked, rough skin on fingers (also antisynthetase)
  • Calcinosis: Especially in juvenile DM

Muscle Involvement

  • Proximal weakness: Difficulty rising from chair, climbing stairs, lifting arms
  • Symmetric
  • CK: Elevated (often 10-50√ó normal)
  • Aldolase: Also elevated
Malignancy association: DM has 3-8√ó increased cancer risk. Screen with age-appropriate cancer screening + CT chest/abdomen/pelvis. Highest risk in first 1-3 years after diagnosis.

Polymyositis

  • Similar muscle involvement as DM
  • NO skin manifestations
  • Rare diagnosis ‚Äî many former PM cases now reclassified as IMNM or antisynthetase

Inclusion Body Myositis

  • Age: >50 years, M > F
  • Distribution: Asymmetric, involves finger flexors and quadriceps (falls, difficulty gripping)
  • CK: Normal to mildly elevated
  • Treatment: Does NOT respond to immunosuppression
  • Biopsy: Rimmed vacuoles, inclusion bodies
IBM is the most common myopathy in patients >50. Key clue: weakness of finger flexors (difficulty gripping) + quadriceps weakness + poor response to steroids.

  • Antibody: Anti-Jo-1 (most common), anti-PL-7, anti-PL-12, others
  • Classic features: Myositis + ILD + arthritis + mechanic's hands + Raynaud's + fever
  • ILD: Can be severe and progressive

9. Antiphospholipid Syndrome Boards Favorite

Definition

Autoimmune thrombophilia characterized by arterial/venous thrombosis and/or pregnancy morbidity with persistent antiphospholipid antibodies.

Clinical Manifestations

Thrombosis

  • Venous (most common): DVT, PE, cerebral venous sinus thrombosis
  • Arterial: Stroke, TIA, MI (often in young patients)
  • Unusual sites: Hepatic veins (Budd-Chiari), renal, adrenal

Pregnancy Complications

  • Recurrent early miscarriages (<10 weeks)
  • Late fetal loss (>10 weeks)
  • Preterm delivery due to preeclampsia/placental insufficiency

Other Manifestations

  • Livedo reticularis
  • Thrombocytopenia (moderate, usually >50k)
  • Cardiac valve disease (Libman-Sacks endocarditis)
  • Nephropathy (thrombotic microangiopathy)

10. Sj√∂gren's Syndrome

Overview

  • Definition: Chronic autoimmune disease targeting exocrine glands (salivary, lacrimal)
  • Demographics: F:M = 9:1, peak 40-60 years
  • Types: Primary (alone) vs Secondary (with RA, SLE, scleroderma)

Clinical Features

Sicca Symptoms (Hallmark)

  • Keratoconjunctivitis sicca: Dry eyes, gritty sensation, photosensitivity
  • Xerostomia: Dry mouth, difficulty swallowing dry foods, dental caries
  • Parotid enlargement: Bilateral, episodic

Extraglandular Manifestations

SystemManifestation
JointsArthralgias, non-erosive arthritis
SkinPurpura, xerosis, annular erythema
PulmonaryILD, bronchiectasis, lymphocytic interstitial pneumonia
RenalInterstitial nephritis, RTA type 1 (distal)
NeurologicPeripheral neuropathy, CNS involvement (rare)
HematologicLeukopenia, hypergammaglobulinemia, cryoglobulinemia
Lymphoma risk: 5-10% lifetime risk of B-cell lymphoma (especially MALT). Monitor for persistent parotid swelling, lymphadenopathy, cryoglobulinemia.

Diagnosis

Serologies

  • Anti-Ro (SSA): 60-70% sensitive
  • Anti-La (SSB): 30-40% sensitive, usually with anti-Ro
  • RF: Positive in 50-70%
  • ANA: Positive in 80%

Objective Tests

  • Schirmer test: Filter paper in eye, <5mm wetting in 5 min = abnormal
  • Ocular staining: Rose bengal, lissamine green
  • Salivary flow: Unstimulated flow <0.1mL/min abnormal
  • Minor salivary gland biopsy: Focal lymphocytic sialadenitis (focus score ‚â•1)

Treatment

  • Dry eyes: Artificial tears, cyclosporine drops (Restasis), punctal plugs
  • Dry mouth: Frequent sips of water, sugar-free gum, pilocarpine, cevimeline
  • Dental care: Fluoride, regular dental visits
  • Systemic disease: HCQ for arthralgias, fatigue; immunosuppressants for severe organ involvement

20. Board-Style Practice Questions

Question 1

A 55-year-old woman presents with 3 months of bilateral shoulder and hip pain with morning stiffness lasting 2 hours. ESR is 85 mm/h. She reports new headaches for 1 week. What is the next best step?

Answer: Start high-dose prednisone (40-60mg) immediately and arrange temporal artery biopsy. The new headache in a PMR patient suggests possible GCA ‚Äî a medical emergency that can cause irreversible vision loss.

Question 2

A 35-year-old man with a 5-year history of inflammatory back pain presents with acute, painful red eye. What is the most likely diagnosis and what is the expected HLA finding?

Answer: Acute anterior uveitis in the setting of ankylosing spondylitis. HLA-B27 would be expected positive in >90% of AS patients and >50% of isolated AAU cases.

Question 3

A 28-year-old woman with SLE and positive antiphospholipid antibodies has her first DVT. What is the recommended anticoagulation?

Answer: Warfarin with INR target 2-3, indefinitely. DOACs should NOT be used in APS (TRAPS trial showed increased thrombosis with rivaroxaban).

Question 4

A patient with diffuse cutaneous systemic sclerosis develops acute hypertension (BP 210/120) and AKI. What is the diagnosis and first-line treatment?

Answer: Scleroderma renal crisis. First-line treatment is ACE inhibitor (captopril), even if creatinine continues to rise initially.

Question 5

A 65-year-old man has acute monoarthritis of the first MTP joint. Serum uric acid is 6.2 mg/dL (normal). Does this rule out gout?

Answer: No. Serum uric acid can be normal during acute gout attacks. The gold standard diagnosis is arthrocentesis showing negatively birefringent, needle-shaped monosodium urate crystals.

Question 6

A patient with GPA (granulomatosis with polyangiitis) has pulmonary hemorrhage and RPGN. What is the recommended induction therapy?

Answer: High-dose IV methylprednisolone + rituximab (or cyclophosphamide). Consider plasma exchange for severe pulmonary hemorrhage or dialysis-dependent renal failure.
